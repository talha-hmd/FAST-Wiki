FAST Merit & Cutoffs — Why No One Can Predict Your Odds

FAST merit is one of the most confusing parts of the admission process because students keep asking “Can I get in with this aggregate?” The honest answer is that no one can predict it perfectly. You can only estimate your chances using previous data, your campus/program target, and a safe buffer.

FAST Does Not Publish a Ranked Merit List

FAST usually does not publish a simple ranked list where you can see every applicant's position. Instead, the result is shown through statuses like Selected, Eligible, or Denied for specific campus and program choices.

This is why most “closing merit” numbers online are not official. They are usually reconstructed from student screenshots, self-reported outcomes, community spreadsheets, or admission discussion groups.

Selected

Your aggregate cleared the requirement for that campus/program option.

Eligible

You may be considered for another preference or available seat depending on the situation.

Denied

Your aggregate did not clear the requirement for that option in that admission cycle.

Important: Treat online closing merit numbers as estimates, not official proof. They are useful for planning, but they are not guaranteed.

How to Estimate Your Own Chances

The safest way to estimate your chances is to compare your aggregate with previous unofficial closing merits for the exact campus and program you are targeting.

Do not compare Lahore CS with another campus or another program and assume the result will be the same. FAST merit depends heavily on campus, program, seat pool, and applicant demand.

1

Find unofficial closing merit data from the last 2 to 3 admission cycles.

2

Look only at your exact campus and program combination.

3

Add a safe buffer of around 2 to 3 percent above the previous closing merit.

4

Compare your calculated aggregate with that buffered number.

5

If you are below the safe range, apply to backup universities immediately.

Why Cutoffs Move Every Year

Merit cutoffs are not fixed. They can move up or down every year depending on seats, applicants, test performance, board results, and changes in programs or campuses.

Seat Supply

New campuses, new programs, or more seats can reduce pressure and make merit more relaxed.

Applicant Demand

If more high-scoring students apply for the same campus/program, closing merit can rise.

Board Results

If intermediate results are generally lower in a year, academic-record aggregates may also shift.

NAT cutoffs can be especially unstable because the reserved pool is small. A small change in applicants or seats can create a large change in closing merit.

Why “Chance Me” Posts Are Usually Not Useful

Students often post their marks online and ask strangers if they can get into FAST. The problem is that strangers do not know the final applicant pool, seat count, program demand, or the exact closing merit for that year.

At best, they can give you an estimate. At worst, they can give you false confidence or unnecessary stress.

Better approach: Calculate your aggregate, compare it with previous unofficial data, add a 2 to 3 percent buffer, and keep backup options ready.

The Mental Model

Once applications close and the applicant pool is fixed, your result depends on the numbers. Stressing over the result will not change the merit chart.

The only productive thing you can do during the waiting period is secure alternatives. Apply to backup universities, understand refund deadlines, and keep your documents ready.

  • If your aggregate is clearly above the safe range, you are probably in a good position.
  • If your aggregate is near the previous closing merit, treat it as uncertain.
  • If your aggregate is below the buffered number, do not wait blindly. Apply elsewhere too.

Quick Summary

FAST does not publish a clean ranked merit list, so most closing merit numbers online are unofficial estimates. Use them carefully, but do not treat them as final truth.

The safest method is to check previous years, focus on your exact campus/program, add a 2 to 3 percent buffer, and keep backup universities ready.
