The Teacher-Quality Lottery at FAST — How to Handle It

At FAST, your semester experience can change a lot depending on which teacher you get. Some instructors explain from the basics and support students properly. Others move fast, rely heavily on slides, or mark very strictly. The key is to understand the lottery and learn how to survive it without letting one bad allocation destroy your semester.

The Teacher Lottery

Teacher allocation matters because two students can take the same course in the same semester and still have very different experiences. One section may get a teacher who explains concepts clearly, while another section may get someone who expects students to self-study most of the course.

This does not mean every teacher is bad. It means you should not rely only on the assigned teacher to carry your learning. At FAST, self-study and external resources are part of survival.

Different Marking

Self-Study Needed

A weak teacher means you must depend more on resources, seniors, and practice.

Simple truth: At FAST, do not assume your teacher will be your only source of learning. Always keep backup resources ready.

The Three Teacher Types

FAST students often describe teachers in three broad categories. These are not official categories, but they help explain the student experience.

The Great Teacher

Explains from the ground up, answers questions patiently, gives useful examples, and makes difficult topics feel manageable.

The Decent Teacher

Covers the course properly but may follow a fixed lecture plan, answer limited questions, or expect students to do extra practice themselves.

The Poor Teacher

Relies too much on slides, explains weakly, rushes through content, and may still mark strictly.

Your strategy should change depending on the teacher type. With a great teacher, attend carefully and ask questions. With a decent teacher, supplement with practice. With a poor teacher, immediately build your own learning system outside class.

Why Weak Teachers Hurt More at FAST

A weak teacher is frustrating anywhere, but at FAST it hurts more because the pace is already intense. Quizzes, assignments, labs, sessionals, and finals keep moving even if the class did not understand the topic properly.

The problem becomes worse when a weak teacher also marks strictly. Students then feel they are being taught lightly but assessed harshly.

  • The semester pace does not slow down for weak teaching.
  • Quizzes can happen before students feel ready.
  • Assignments may require concepts not explained deeply in class.
  • Strict marking can punish small misunderstandings.
  • Students may need to self-study while still handling normal FAST workload.
Reality check: If your teacher is weak, waiting for the course to magically become clear is risky. Start external learning early.

The Strategic Response

The worst response to a weak teacher is only complaining. Complaining may be valid, but it does not teach you the course. The better response is to build a backup system quickly.

Use YouTube playlists, senior notes, past papers, classmates, textbooks, and AI tools to understand the course before quizzes and sessionals expose your gaps.

1

Identify early whether the teacher's explanation is enough for you.

2

Ask seniors which resources match that course and teacher style.

3

Follow one reliable external resource instead of collecting ten playlists.

4

Practice past papers, assignments, and quiz-style questions.

5

Keep attending class anyway because teacher hints and grading expectations still matter.

Do Not Stop Attending Class

Even if the teacher is weak, skipping class completely is usually a bad idea. At FAST, attendance matters, and teachers often reveal quiz topics, assignment expectations, checking style, and important announcements during class.

You may learn the concept from outside, but class still tells you what the teacher cares about. That can matter a lot in quizzes, sessionals, and assignments.

Attendance

Teacher Hints

Best approach: Learn from outside if needed, but keep attending class for attendance, hints, and teacher-specific expectations.

When the Whole Class Has a Problem

If only one or two students are struggling, the issue may be personal preparation. But if the entire class agrees that teaching is seriously inadequate, the class can escalate respectfully.

The correct route is not public drama or insulting posts. The better route is a calm, collective, respectful complaint to the HOD with specific issues.

  • Collect specific problems instead of vague anger.
  • Mention missed topics, unclear lectures, or assessment mismatch.
  • Keep the tone respectful and academic.
  • Have class representatives or responsible students communicate.
  • Request support, clarification, or teacher change if the case is serious.
Important: Escalation should be respectful and evidence-based. Venting publicly usually creates more problems than solutions.

The Small Injustices

Some of the most frustrating FAST experiences are small marking or submission injustices. A late upload, unclear instruction, strict rubric, or minor format issue can sometimes cost marks.

These situations feel unfair because they are often small, but they still affect grades. The best protection is to make your own work and submissions as clean as possible.

Problem Why It Hurts How to Protect Yourself
Late submission Even a small delay can cause deduction or rejection. Submit early and keep proof.
Unclear instructions Students may lose marks for misunderstanding requirements. Ask questions in writing when instructions are unclear.
Strict marking Small mistakes can cost more than expected. Follow teacher format and past marking style.
Technical upload issue The teacher may not accept excuses later. Take screenshots and email immediately if something breaks.

How to Stay Safe With Submissions

Many students lose marks because of careless submission habits. At FAST, you should treat every assignment or lab submission like a small official record.

  • Submit before the last minute.
  • Keep screenshots of successful submissions.
  • Email the teacher or TA immediately if the portal has an issue.
  • Follow file naming instructions exactly.
  • Do not submit corrupted files.
  • Check whether the uploaded file opens properly.
  • Keep backup copies of assignments and code.
  • Read the rubric before submitting, not after marks are released.
Simple rule: Make your work watertight. The less room for technical excuses or ambiguity, the safer your marks are.

How Seniors Can Help

Seniors are one of the best sources for handling teacher-specific problems. They can tell you which topics the teacher emphasizes, how strict the marking is, what past papers looked like, and which resources helped them.

Do not wait until finals to ask seniors. Ask early in the semester so you can adjust your strategy before sessionals.

Past Papers

Seniors can guide you toward the kind of questions usually asked.

Teacher Style

They can explain whether the teacher prefers theory, coding, derivations, or exact formatting.

Resources

They can suggest playlists, notes, books, or practice sets that actually match the course.

Common Mistakes Students Make

Teacher problems become worse when students respond emotionally instead of strategically.

  • Complaining all semester but not self-studying.
  • Skipping class because the teacher is weak.
  • Waiting until sessionals to find external resources.
  • Ignoring teacher-specific marking style.
  • Not asking seniors for guidance early.
  • Submitting assignments at the last minute.
  • Escalating rudely instead of respectfully.
  • Assuming a weak teacher means the exam will also be easy.

The Balanced Truth

Teacher quality at FAST can be uneven. Some teachers are excellent, some are manageable, and some force students to self-study much more than they expected.

But one weak teacher does not have to ruin your semester if you react early. Attend class, learn externally, ask seniors, practice past papers, keep submissions clean, and escalate respectfully only when the whole class has a serious issue.

Final view: You cannot control teacher allocation, but you can control your response. At FAST, that response matters a lot.

Quick Summary

FAST has a teacher-quality lottery. A great teacher can make a hard course easier, while a weak teacher can make even a normal course stressful.

The safest strategy is to identify the issue early, keep attending class, use external resources, ask seniors, protect your submissions, and escalate respectfully if the whole class is genuinely affected.
