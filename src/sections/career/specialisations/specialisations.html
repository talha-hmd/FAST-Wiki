Career & Future

FAANG, Cybersecurity & Specialisation — Niche Paths from FAST

FAST can open strong local tech opportunities, but niche paths like FAANG, cybersecurity, AI, data science, and global big-tech roles need much more than a university tag. These paths are possible, but they require focused skills, projects, networking, luck, and early planning.

FAANG and Global Big-Tech Direct Hire

Direct hiring into global big-tech companies from Pakistan is rare, but not impossible. FAST students and graduates have reached major international companies before, but those cases usually involve a mix of strong skills, timing, network, interview preparation, and luck.

The key mistake is to treat FAANG as a default outcome. It should be treated as a high-upside target, not the only plan.

Rare

Direct global big-tech hiring is not the normal outcome for most students.

Real

It has happened, so the path exists for exceptional profiles.

Unreliable

Luck, market timing, referrals, and hiring cycles matter a lot.

Simple truth: Build like you want FAANG, but do not make your entire career plan depend on FAANG.

What Actually Helps for Big Tech

Big-tech hiring usually cares about problem solving, fundamentals, interview performance, projects, internships, open-source work, and referrals. The university name can help create credibility, but it does not replace preparation.

Students who want this path should start early. Waiting until final year to learn DSA, system design basics, GitHub, and interview preparation makes the path much harder.

1

First year: Build strong programming fundamentals and do assignments yourself.

2

Second year: Learn DSA properly and begin structured problem-solving practice.

3

Second summer: Build complete projects and clean up GitHub.

4

Third year: Target internships, open-source, hackathons, and referrals.

5

Final year: Prepare seriously for interviews and apply broadly.

Do Not Chase FAANG Blindly

FAANG sounds attractive because of salaries, prestige, and international exposure. But if you only chase the label, you may ignore more realistic and equally valuable opportunities: strong local software houses, remote jobs, startups, product companies, research roles, and skilled migration routes.

A better approach is to build strong fundamentals and a strong profile. That profile can help with FAANG, but it also helps with every other serious tech opportunity.

  • Practice DSA after understanding DSA concepts properly.
  • Build projects that solve real problems.
  • Improve GitHub, CV, and LinkedIn.
  • Ask seniors and alumni for realistic guidance.
  • Do internships early if possible.
  • Apply to strong local and remote roles too.
  • Do not wait for one dream company to validate your career.
Better mindset: Prepare for top companies, but build a career that does not collapse if top companies do not respond.

Cybersecurity Specialisation

Cybersecurity is an interesting and valuable field, but it is more niche than many students expect. The local entry-level cybersecurity market can be thinner than general software engineering, and many roles require practical depth, certifications, networking, or specialised experience.

If you are genuinely passionate about security, the path can be worth it. But if you are choosing cybersecurity only because it sounds cool or trendy, BS CS is usually the safer option.

Cybersecurity

Good for students who are genuinely interested in security, networks, systems, and investigation.

Entry Market

Can be narrower locally compared to regular software engineering roles.

Safer Pivot

BS CS keeps cybersecurity open while also preserving software, AI, data, and systems paths.

Important: Do not specialise in cybersecurity unless you have researched the field and actually enjoy the work.

CS vs Cybersecurity

If you are unsure, BS Computer Science is usually the safer degree. CS gives you a broad foundation and keeps more doors open. You can still move into cybersecurity later through electives, projects, certifications, labs, CTFs, internships, and self-study.

A narrow degree can be good when you are already sure about the niche. But if you are still exploring, choosing a broader program reduces regret.

Factor BS Computer Science BS Cybersecurity
Career Flexibility Very broad: software, AI, data, systems, web, cloud, security. More focused toward security-related roles.
Best For Students who are unsure or want maximum optionality. Students who have confirmed interest in security.
Local Entry-Level Market Broader and usually easier to enter. Narrower and more specialised.
Pivoting Later Can pivot into cybersecurity later. Can pivot out, but may require more self-study for general software roles.
Rule: When uncertain, choose the broader degree. Specialise after you understand what the field actually requires.

Other Specialisation Paths

Cybersecurity is not the only niche. FAST students also explore AI, data science, web development, mobile development, cloud, DevOps, game development, product engineering, and systems programming.

The best specialisation is not the one with the most hype. The best specialisation is the one where your interest, market demand, skill growth, and project opportunities overlap.

Path Good For Main Warning
Software Engineering Students who want broad local and remote job opportunities. Requires projects, practical stack, and interview preparation.
AI / Machine Learning Students who enjoy math, data, models, and experimentation. Real AI work needs strong math, Python, data, and patience.
Data Science Students who like statistics, analytics, dashboards, and business problems. Entry-level data roles can be competitive and require portfolio proof.
Cybersecurity Students who enjoy security, networks, systems, and ethical hacking. Do not choose it only because it sounds cool.
Cloud / DevOps Students interested in deployment, infrastructure, automation, and reliability. Usually easier after learning backend and Linux basics.
Systems Students who like OS, compilers, low-level programming, and performance. Harder path, but valuable for deep technical roles.

How to Test a Specialisation Before Choosing It

You do not need to decide your whole career from one YouTube video or one senior's opinion. Test a niche before committing to it seriously.

A good test is simple: spend four to six weeks learning the basics of that field and build one small project. If the work still interests you after the hype fades, the path may be worth exploring further.

1

Watch a beginner roadmap or course overview for the field.

2

Learn the basic tools and vocabulary.

3

Build one small project or lab.

4

Talk to a senior, graduate, or professional already in that field.

5

Decide based on actual experience, not vibes only.

Specialisation vs Employability

A specialisation only helps if it increases your employability or matches your long-term direction. A trendy label without skills does not help much.

For most students, the safest early-career path is to build a strong base first: programming, DSA, databases, operating systems, networking basics, Git, communication, and one practical stack.

  • Learn programming fundamentals properly.
  • Study DSA before heavy Leetcode.
  • Understand databases and backend basics.
  • Build complete projects, not only tutorials.
  • Learn Git, GitHub, and deployment.
  • Improve communication and interview explanation.
  • Then move deeper into a niche.
Best order: Foundation first, specialisation second.

When a Specialised Degree Makes Sense

A specialised degree can make sense when you have already researched the field, understand the job market, and genuinely enjoy the work.

For example, cybersecurity can make sense if you enjoy networks, Linux, security labs, CTFs, incident analysis, malware basics, and ethical hacking. AI can make sense if you enjoy math, Python, data, models, and research-like experimentation.

You Researched It

You know what jobs exist, what skills are needed, and what entry-level work looks like.

You Practiced It

You have tried projects, labs, or courses and still like the field.

You Accept the Risk

You understand the narrower path and still prefer it.

When BS CS Is the Better Choice

BS CS is usually better when you are still unsure. It gives you more flexibility and lets you explore software engineering, AI, data, cybersecurity, cloud, research, and product development before locking yourself into one identity.

This flexibility matters because many students change their interests after studying real courses and building real projects.

  • Choose BS CS if you are unsure between multiple fields.
  • Choose BS CS if your main goal is broad employability.
  • Choose BS CS if you want to keep abroad, local jobs, and remote work open.
  • Choose BS CS if you are only attracted to a niche because of hype.
  • Choose BS CS if the specialised program is more expensive or less established.
Final rule: CS keeps doors open. A niche closes some doors in exchange for depth. Only make that trade when you are sure.

Common Mistakes Students Make

Many students choose specialisations based on trends, fear, or social media instead of actual field experience.

  • Choosing cybersecurity only because it sounds cool.
  • Choosing AI without liking math, data, or Python.
  • Thinking FAANG is a normal guaranteed path.
  • Ignoring local and remote job markets while chasing prestige only.
  • Starting Leetcode before properly learning DSA.
  • Collecting roadmaps but not building projects.
  • Choosing a narrow degree while still confused.
  • Assuming the university tag can replace skill depth.

The Balanced Truth

FAST can support strong career paths, but niche outcomes require more than surviving the degree. FAANG and global big-tech are possible but rare. Cybersecurity is valuable but narrower. AI and data are powerful but need serious math and project depth.

If you know your niche, build toward it seriously. If you are unsure, choose the broader path, build strong fundamentals, and specialise through projects, electives, internships, and self-study.

Final view: Do not choose a niche for the title. Choose it because you understand the work and still want to do it.

Quick Summary

FAANG and global big-tech hiring from FAST is rare but real. It depends on strong skills, interview preparation, referrals, projects, timing, and luck. Do not bank on it as your only plan.

Cybersecurity is a niche path with a thinner local entry-level market, so confirm your passion before specialising. When uncertain, BS CS is usually the safest choice because it keeps software, AI, data, cybersecurity, cloud, systems, local jobs, remote work, and abroad options open.
