FAST Fee Structure — What You Actually Pay Per Semester

FAST is not only about admission merit. You also need to understand the actual cost of studying there. The fee is more than just semester tuition. Admission fee, security deposit, hostel, transport, repeat courses, summer courses, and small administrative charges can all affect your real budget.

The Headline Components

When students talk about FAST fees, they usually focus only on tuition. That is a mistake. The real cost includes one-time charges, recurring semester charges, and possible extra costs during the degree.

Admission Fee

A one-time processing or admission-related fee paid at the time of admission. Usually non-refundable.

Security Deposit

A refundable amount paid once, usually returned when you leave the university after clearing dues.

Tuition Fee

The main semester fee. It varies by program, campus, and current fee cycle.

Important: FAST fee figures revise annually. Before publishing or making a financial decision, verify every amount from the live FAST fee structure page.

What You May Actually Pay For

A realistic budget should include more than tuition. Some charges happen once, some happen every semester, and some appear only if you repeat a course, take summer, live in hostel, or miss a fee deadline.

Cost Type What It Means When It Matters
Admission / Processing Fee One-time fee paid during admission. At the start, usually non-refundable.
Security Deposit Refundable deposit after leaving university and clearing dues. Paid at admission.
Semester Tuition Main academic fee for the semester. Every Fall and Spring semester.
Per-Credit-Hour Fee Fee calculated based on course credit hours. Important for repeats, summer, and extra courses.
Hostel Accommodation cost for students living away from home. Important for outstation students.
Miscellaneous Charges Administrative, departmental, transcript, migration, or other small charges. Can appear during the degree.

The Incidental Charges That Aren't in the Headline

The painful part of university affordability is often not the headline fee. It is the unexpected extra charges that show up during the semester.

These may include late-payment fines, registration suspension issues, repeat-course billing, summer course fees, transcript charges, migration charges, or other administrative fees.

  • Late-payment fines
  • Registration suspension triggers
  • Repeat-course fees
  • Summer semester fees
  • Transcript charges
  • Migration or administrative charges
  • Hostel and transport-related expenses
  • Extra cost from failed or withdrawn courses
Budget rule: Do not read only the tuition line. Read the complete fee structure page because small charges can become stressful later.

Indicative Year-One Outlay

For computing programs, students often estimate FAST tuition at roughly a few lakh rupees across two semesters, excluding hostel. But exact numbers vary by year, campus, program, and official fee revision.

A safer way to think is this: calculate the full first-year cost before aid, then calculate the same cost after financial assistance or scholarships. Also add hostel or transport if you are not living near campus.

With Repeats

Why Fee Planning Matters at FAST

FAST's academic pressure means students should financially plan for the possibility of repeats, summer courses, or delayed progression. This does not mean every student will fail courses. It means the budget should not be so tight that one repeat creates a crisis.

If your family can only barely afford the normal semester fee, then repeat fees, hostel expenses, or delayed scholarship disbursement can become a serious problem.

  • Ask whether your family can handle one unexpected extra fee.
  • Check whether hostel or transport is required.
  • Understand how financial assistance works before depending on it.
  • Keep a backup university option if FAST requires risky borrowing.
  • Do not ignore repeat and summer-course costs.

The Refund Window

Refund policy matters when you are holding a FAST offer while waiting for another university. For example, a student may submit FAST's fee to secure a seat while waiting for NUST, LUMS, or another offer.

The earlier you withdraw, the more fee you may recover. The admission or processing fee is usually non-refundable, so do not include it in your expected recovery.

1

Up to day 10: Usually the highest refund window.

2

Up to day 15: Refund percentage usually starts reducing.

3

Up to day 20: Refund amount reduces further.

4

Up to day 30: Usually the final meaningful refund window.

5

After day 30: Usually no refund.

Practical advice: If you deposit the fee to secure a seat, track refund dates carefully. Missing the refund window can cost a lot.

How to Build Your FAST Budget

The best way to decide affordability is to build a simple four-year estimate. Do not only ask, “Can we pay the first semester?” Ask whether the full degree is manageable.

1

Check current official semester fee for your program and campus.

2

Add admission fee, security deposit, and miscellaneous charges.

3

Add hostel, transport, food, and personal expenses if needed.

4

Add a small safety margin for repeats, summer, or delayed aid.

5

Compare the total with financial assistance, scholarships, and backup university costs.

Common Mistakes Students Make

Most fee-related stress happens because students calculate only the visible semester fee and ignore the full degree cost.

  • Ignoring hostel and transport cost.
  • Assuming financial assistance will arrive before the first payment.
  • Forgetting that admission processing fee is usually non-refundable.
  • Not checking repeat-course and summer-course costs.
  • Missing refund deadlines while waiting for another university.
  • Using old fee screenshots instead of the live official fee page.
  • Choosing FAST without comparing total four-year cost with backups.

Quick Summary

FAST's real cost includes more than tuition. You need to consider admission fee, security deposit, semester fee, hostel, transport, miscellaneous charges, repeat fees, summer fees, and refund deadlines.

Before making a decision, verify the official fee structure, build a full-year and four-year estimate, and check whether your family can handle unexpected academic costs like repeats or summer courses.
