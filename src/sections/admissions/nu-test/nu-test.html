The FAST NU Test — Sections, Weighting & How to Prep

The NU Test is FAST's own entrance test and, for most applicants, the safest admission route. This page explains the test sections, which parts matter most for computing applicants, how test-date selection works, and how to prepare without wasting time.

Test Composition

The FAST NU Test usually contains four major sections: Advanced Math, Basic Math, Analytical or IQ Reasoning, and English. These sections test both your academic preparation and your ability to solve problems under time pressure.

Advanced Math

Covers higher-level math concepts. For computing applicants, this is one of the most important sections.

Basic Math

Includes simpler calculations and word problems. The questions may look easy, but speed matters.

Analytical Reasoning

Tests patterns, logic, puzzles, and reasoning. This section can improve a lot with practice.

Important: FAST can revise the pattern or weightings, so always confirm the latest test pattern from the official admissions portal before publishing final numbers.

Indicative Section Weighting

The exact section weightings are not permanently fixed, but the commonly discussed pattern gives a useful idea of where your preparation time should go.

  • Advanced Math — around 30%
  • Basic Math — around 20%
  • Analytical Reasoning — around 30%
  • English — around 20%

Do not memorize these percentages blindly. Use them as a planning guide, then verify the current pattern from FAST's official Test Pattern page before the admission cycle.

Which Sections Decide Your Aggregate?

For most computing applicants, Advanced Math and Analytical Reasoning are the swing sections. These are the areas where strong preparation can create a visible difference in your aggregate.

Basic Math can also become tricky because of word problems. Many students know the formulas but struggle to convert a paragraph into an equation quickly. The solution is not just more calculation practice; it is translation practice.

High Impact

Advanced Math and Analytical Reasoning usually matter the most for improving your score.

Hidden Trap

Basic Math word problems can waste time if you are slow at understanding the question.

Stable Section

English is important, but for many students it is less of a score-changing section than Math and Reasoning.

How Test-Date Selection Works

FAST usually offers multiple NU Test sittings across a date range. During registration, applicants select their test date from the available slots.

Later dates are usually preferred because they give more preparation time. Because of that, they can fill quickly. Keep checking the portal, but do not delay so much that you lose suitable options.

How to Prepare

The NU Test is competitive because of aggregate pressure, not because every question is impossible. A smart preparation plan focuses on the sections that can increase your score the most.

  • Start Advanced Math from your intermediate or calculus textbook.
  • Practice Basic Math word problems by converting statements into equations.
  • Do Analytical Reasoning daily from a dedicated puzzle or reasoning bank.
  • Practice English enough to avoid careless mistakes.
  • Solve at least 2 to 3 full-length mocks under timed conditions.
Best use of time: Do Analytical Reasoning consistently for 4 to 6 weeks. It is one of the most improvable sections if you practice patterns daily.

Common Mistakes Students Make

Many applicants prepare hard but not strategically. They spend too much time on topics they already know and ignore the sections where marks can improve quickly.

  • Preparing only Advanced Math and ignoring Analytical Reasoning.
  • Practicing formulas but not word-problem translation.
  • Not solving timed mocks before the real test.
  • Selecting a test date randomly without checking preparation time.
  • Relying on outdated test-pattern screenshots.

Quick Summary

The NU Test usually has four sections: Advanced Math, Basic Math, Analytical Reasoning, and English. For computing applicants, Advanced Math and Analytical Reasoning usually matter the most.

Your goal is not just to study more. Your goal is to prepare the sections that actually move your aggregate: math, reasoning, word problems, and timed practice.
