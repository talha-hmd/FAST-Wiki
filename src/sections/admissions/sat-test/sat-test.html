FAST SAT Admissions — SAT-I, Separate Merit List & When It Makes Sense

The SAT route can be useful for some FAST applicants, especially students who already have a strong SAT score. But it is not automatically easier than the NU Test. SAT applicants are usually evaluated on a separate merit list, which means the competition works differently.

SAT-II No Longer Exists

SAT Subject Tests, also called SAT-II, were discontinued by the College Board in 2021. So for FAST admissions, the relevant test is SAT-I, not SAT-II.

Some older campus pages or screenshots may still mention SAT-II or show outdated requirements. Do not trust those blindly. Always verify SAT-related rules from FAST's central admissions portal.

Important: If you see a strange SAT rule on an old campus page, treat it as outdated until the central FAST admissions portal confirms it.

How the SAT List Works

SAT applicants are usually placed on a separate merit list. This means they do not compete directly with NU Test applicants in the same pool.

The usual idea is that FAST combines your SAT-I score with your academic record, such as intermediate marks, A-Level equivalence, and matric record. However, the exact formula can change, so it must be verified every admission cycle.

Separate Pool

SAT applicants are usually ranked separately from NU Test applicants.

Academic Record

Your school/intermediate record still matters. SAT alone is not the whole merit.

Higher Competition

Because the SAT pool is smaller, the closing percentage can be higher.

When the SAT Stream Makes Sense

The SAT stream makes sense if you already have a strong SAT score, especially if you are from an A-Level background and had already prepared for SAT anyway.

It is not usually worth starting SAT preparation from zero just for FAST unless you have a clear reason. For most local applicants, the NU Test is still the more practical route because it usually has the larger seat pool.

  • You already have a strong SAT score.
  • You are applying to multiple universities that also accept SAT.
  • You are an A-Level student and SAT already fits your plan.
  • You understand that SAT has a separate and usually more competitive list.

When You Should Prefer the NU Test

If you do not already have a strong SAT score, the NU Test is usually the better choice. FAST's own test generally gives most applicants a more realistic chance because the main seat pool is larger.

This is why many applicants use SAT only as a parallel option, not as their only FAST admission strategy.

Choose NU Test

If you are mainly targeting FAST and have not already prepared for SAT.

Use SAT

If your SAT score is already strong and you want an extra admission route.

Avoid Confusion

Do not assume SAT is easier just because it is internationally accepted.

Verifying SAT Claims

SAT policies can be misunderstood because some old campus pages, screenshots, and student posts may remain online even after rules change. Always verify the current policy from the central FAST admissions portal.

If the portal information still feels unclear, contact the admissions office directly. A direct confirmation is better than relying on outdated posts.

  • Check the central FAST admissions portal.
  • Do not trust old screenshots without verification.
  • Be careful with campus sub-sites that may not be updated.
  • Call the admissions office if the SAT policy is unclear.
Publishing note: Before making this page public for an active admission cycle, re-check the latest SAT policy from the live FAST portal.

Quick Summary

FAST admissions through SAT are based on SAT-I, not SAT-II. SAT applicants usually compete on a separate merit list, and that list can close higher because the pool is smaller.

If you already have a strong SAT score, the SAT stream can be useful. But if you are starting from scratch and mainly targeting FAST, the NU Test is usually the safer default.
