FAST SAT Admissions — SAT-I, Separate Merit List & When It Makes Sense

The SAT route can be useful for some FAST applicants, especially students who already have a strong SAT score. But it is not automatically easier than the NU Test. SAT applicants are evaluated on a separate merit list, which means the competition works differently.

SAT-II (Subject Tests) No Longer Exists

The College Board discontinued SAT-II (Subject Tests) in 2021. Because of this, FAST central admission policy accepts applications via SAT-I only, managing placement through a dedicated, separate merit ranking.

Some campus sub-sites have historically still displayed stale SAT-II references and odd threshold parameters (such as a "1500 minimum"). Assume any odd SAT rule on a campus sub-site is out of date. Always verify the master criteria from the central platform.

Important: If you see a strange SAT rule on an old campus page, treat it as outdated until the central FAST admissions portal confirms it.

How the SAT List Works

SAT applicants are placed on a completely separate merit ranking system. They do not compete directly for the same seat allocations or entry metrics as the standard NU Test applicants.

The structural evaluation framework combines your testing output and high school milestones into a dual calculation weighting:

Separate Pool

Approximately 50% SAT-I performance combined with approximately 50% academic record tracker data.

Academic Record

Includes your high school track (Intermediate marks / A-Level equivalence via IBCC + Matric record metrics).

Higher Competition

Closes higher in percentage terms than the NU Test list because the pool is smaller and more self-selecting.

Data points from a recent unofficial admission cycle highlight how competitive this separate tracking pool can be, with closing aggregates hovering around ~85% for CS-adjacent configurations and peaking around ~86% for Artificial Intelligence (AI) tracks.

When the SAT Stream Makes Sense

The SAT stream provides legitimate parallel play for A-Level students who sat the SAT anyway during their standard cycle and scored exceptionally strong (~1250+ is tough even for A-Level students; a 1300+ score opens up real possibilities).

For most domestic applicants without an existing strong SAT, the NU Test remains the rational default choice. Your NU Test score will clear the larger, main student pool much more easily than your SAT score can clear the smaller, higher-closing separate tracking track.

  • You already hold a strong, validated SAT-I score.
  • You want to run a parallel admission strategy alongside local application formats.
  • You are an A-Level student whose pre-existing academic plan already factored in the SAT.
  • You accept the competitive reality of a much higher closing aggregate threshold.

Verifying SAT Claims

To avoid dead-ends, check every single SAT-related claim or social media tip directly against the master template page at nu.edu.pk/Admissions/EligibilityCriteria.

When in doubt, telephone the admissions office directly. Even university staff are sometimes not fully aware of localized policy shifts until the last minute, meaning a direct call to the branch desk is the only definitive way to close the validation loop safely.

Publishing note: Before making this page public for an active admission cycle, re-check the latest SAT policy from the live FAST portal.

Quick Summary

FAST admissions through SAT are based on SAT-I, not SAT-II. SAT applicants compete on a separate merit list, and that list closes higher because the pool is smaller and more competitive.

If you already have a strong SAT score, the SAT stream can be useful. But if you are starting from scratch and mainly targeting FAST, the NU Test is always the safer default.
