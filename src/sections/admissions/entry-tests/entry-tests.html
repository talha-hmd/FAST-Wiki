NU Test vs SAT vs NAT for FAST — Which Stream Should You Choose?

One of the biggest FAST admission decisions is not just which campus or program you choose. It is also which admission stream you apply through. The NU Test, SAT, and NTS NAT do not compete in the exact same pool, so choosing the wrong stream can make admission much harder than it needs to be.

The Core Decision: Which Pool Do You Compete In?

Your single most consequential admissions choice is the stream, because each competes for a different seat pool with a different closing merit. Every admission stream has its own competition level—your score is never judged in isolation, but against the specific applicants applying through that exact same stream.

NU Test

Bulk of seats available. This translates into a more forgiving closing merit, making it the safest default route for the median applicant.

SAT

Smaller, self-selecting pool evaluating on a separate merit list. This results in a higher closing percentage requirement, making it viable only if you already hold a strong SAT score (~1250+ is tough even for A-Level students; 1300+ opens real possibilities).

NTS NAT

Thinnest reserved seat slice. Historically, this stream requires a near-perfect score. The clear advice here is: "Do not ever go for NTS NAT."

Simple rule: Default to the NU Test. Use the SAT only if you already have a strong score and accept the higher-closing separate list dynamics. Avoid NAT for FAST—it is an easy test, but the tiny seat pool forces a near-perfect aggregate requirement.

The Aggregate Formulas

FAST calculates merit by combining your test scores with your academic profile. While these splits are the commonly reported formulas, the authoritative weightings for the current cycle should always be checked on the official Test Pattern page at nu.edu.pk/Admissions/TestPattern.

  • NU Test & NAT Streams: Calculated using approximately 50% entry test performance + 50% academic record (Intermediate + Matric, or O/A-Level equivalence via IBCC).
  • SAT Stream: Calculated using approximately 50% SAT-I performance + 50% academic record (Intermediate/A-Level-equivalence + Matric). This track is evaluated on a completely separate merit list.

Closing Merit: What the Numbers Looked Like

Because FAST does not publish a simple, public ranked merit list containing raw serial values, closing-merit data floating around online are unofficial reconstructions. They are gathered by the community from student-reported outcomes, screenshots, and self-reported spreadsheets.

Data from a recent unofficial cycle highlights how drastically the requirements diverge based on the seat pool:

1

NU Test (Lahore CS): The closing aggregate landed in the low-to-mid 70s (~73% was cited one year), highlighting why it is the most forgiving option.

2

SAT Merit List: Closed at approximately ~85% for CS-adjacent programs and ~86% for Artificial Intelligence (AI).

3

NAT Stream: Demands a near-perfect aggregate and swings violently due to the small pool—witnessing a ~+10% jump one year followed by a ~-8% drop the next.

This is why you should never judge an admission stream solely by the difficulty of the paper. A slightly harder test with thousands of available seats is always safer than an easier test with a paper-thin seat allocation.

The Verdict

For the vast majority of applicants, the smartest and most practical choice is to choose the NU Test stream. FAST heavily "prefers its own test" in practice simply because that is where the vast bulk of the university's seats are allocated.

Final recommendation: Default to the NU Test. Use the SAT track only if you already hold a stellar score and accept the competitive nature of the separate list. Avoid the NAT stream entirely as a core admission strategy unless your score is near-perfect.
