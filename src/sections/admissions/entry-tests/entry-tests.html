NU Test vs SAT vs NAT for FAST — Which Stream Should You Choose?

One of the biggest FAST admission decisions is not just which campus or program you choose. It is also which admission stream you apply through. The NU Test, SAT, and NTS NAT do not compete in the exact same pool, so choosing the wrong stream can make admission much harder than it needs to be.

The Core Decision: Which Pool Do You Compete In?

Every admission stream has its own competition level. This means your score is not judged in isolation. It is judged against the applicants applying through the same stream.

For most applicants, the NU Test is the safest option because it usually has the largest seat pool. SAT can work if you already have a strong score, while NAT is usually risky because the reserved pool is much smaller.

NU Test

FAST's own entrance test. Usually the best default choice because the seat pool is larger and the closing merit is generally more forgiving.

SAT

A separate merit-list route. Useful mainly if you already have a strong SAT score, but the pool is smaller and usually more competitive.

NTS NAT

Usually the riskiest route for FAST. The test may feel easier, but the reserved seat pool is small, so the required score can become extremely high.

Simple rule: If you are confused, prepare for the NU Test. Do not choose NAT just because the paper looks easier.

NU Test — The Recommended Default

The NU Test is FAST's own admission test, and for most students it is the most practical route. It usually has the largest seat pool, which makes it more forgiving compared to the other streams.

This does not mean the NU Test is easy. It means your admission chances are usually better here because more seats are available through this route.

  • Best default option for most local applicants.
  • Usually has the largest seat pool.
  • Good choice if you are applying mainly to FAST.
  • Requires proper preparation, especially for math and analytical reasoning.

SAT Stream — Good Only If Your Score Is Strong

The SAT stream can be useful, especially for students who already prepared for the SAT or come from an A-Level background. But SAT applicants are usually evaluated on a separate merit list, so you are not directly competing with the NU Test pool.

Because this pool is smaller and more self-selecting, the closing percentage can be higher. A normal SAT score may not be enough. You usually need a strong score to make this route worth considering.

  • Useful if you already have a strong SAT score.
  • Usually evaluated separately from NU Test applicants.
  • Can be competitive because the pool is smaller.
  • Not the best route if you have not already prepared for SAT.
Important: SAT rules can change, and some campus pages may show outdated information. Always verify SAT-related policy from the central FAST admissions portal.

NTS NAT — Why It Is Usually Risky

NAT creates the most confusion because many students think an easier test automatically means easier admission. That is not how it works. Admission depends on the pool, not just the difficulty of the paper.

If the NAT seat pool is very small, then even a good NAT score may not be enough. This is why NAT is usually not recommended for FAST unless your score is extremely strong.

  • Small reserved seat pool.
  • Can require a near-perfect score.
  • Closing merit can swing heavily from year to year.
  • Risky as your main FAST admission strategy.

The Aggregate Formulas

FAST usually calculates merit by combining your test score with your academic record. The exact formula can change between admission cycles, so this page should be treated as a guide, not the final rule.

NU Test

Usually around 50% entry test and 50% academic record. Confirm the exact split from the official portal.

SAT

Usually around 50% SAT-I and 50% academic record, evaluated on a separate merit list.

NAT

Usually similar to NU Test in structure, but the smaller seat pool makes it much less forgiving.

Verify before publishing: Aggregate weightings can change. Before final deployment, confirm the current formula from the live FAST admissions portal.

Closing Merit: What the Numbers Usually Show

FAST does not always publish a clean ranked merit list, so most closing-merit numbers online are unofficial. They are usually reconstructed from student-reported results, screenshots, and community spreadsheets.

Still, the pattern is useful. NU Test usually remains the most forgiving route, SAT tends to close higher, and NAT can become extremely unpredictable because of its smaller pool.

1

NU Test: Usually the safest route because more seats are available.

2

SAT: Can close higher because the applicants are competing in a smaller separate list.

3

NAT: Can require a very high or near-perfect score because the reserved pool is thin.

This is why you should never judge a stream only by paper difficulty. A slightly harder test with more seats can be safer than an easier test with very few seats.

The Verdict

For most applicants, the best strategy is to choose the NU Test as the main route. It gives you the most practical chance because FAST usually fills the largest portion of seats through its own test.

SAT is useful only if you already have a strong score and understand that you are competing on a separate list. NAT should usually be avoided as the main strategy unless your NAT score is extremely high.

Final recommendation: Default to the NU Test. Use SAT only with a strong existing score. Avoid NAT unless your NAT score is near-perfect.
