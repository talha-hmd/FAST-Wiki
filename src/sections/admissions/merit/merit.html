FAST Merit & Cutoffs — Why No One Can Predict Your Odds

FAST merit is one of the most confusing parts of the admission process because students keep asking “Can I get in with this aggregate?” The honest answer is that no one can predict it perfectly. You can only estimate your chances using previous data, your campus/program target, and a safe buffer.

FAST Does Not Publish a Ranked Merit List

FAST completely avoids publishing a simple ranked list containing raw student placements or absolute positions. Instead, the institution updates its system by releasing a Selected / Eligible / Denied status chart matching every distinct campus-and-program intersection.

Consequently, every single "closing merit" number floating online is completely unofficial. These figures are reconstructed by the community from self-reported outcomes, student screenshots, and communal spreadsheets—treat them strictly as an approximate estimate, not as hard fact.

Selected

Your aggregate successfully cleared the cutoff boundary condition for that campus/program option.

Eligible

You met the criteria for a lower preference choice and remain bound to that selection pending seat availability.

Denied

Your computed aggregate score sat entirely below the minimum closing requirement for that specific cycle.

Important: Treat online closing merit numbers as estimates, not official proof. They are useful for planning, but they are not guaranteed.

How to Estimate Your Own Chances

Because FAST merit is highly localized and depends entirely on the unique demand of each campus-and-program combination, you should follow a systematic estimation model rather than comparing unrelated data points:

1

Pull 2–3 years of unofficial merit lists from circulating community folders and aggregate calculators.

2

Isolate the specific closing aggregate required for your target campus-and-program combination.

3

Add +2–3% onto that baseline as a worst-case buffer to account for annual aggregate shifts.

4

Compare your actual computed aggregate directly against that buffered target score.

5

Clearing it means you are plausibly safe (never certain); sitting below it means you must apply to a backup university immediately.

Why Cutoffs Move Every Year

Merit boundaries are fluid structures that shift continuously across cycles based on macroscopic variables within the candidate pools:

Seat Supply Shifts

Launching a new campus, opening a new program (such as AI arriving at Lahore), or experiencing a surge in SAT-stream admissions relaxes the NU Test closing merit by expanding the pool.

National Passing Ratios

A cycle featuring exceptionally low intermediate pass rates compresses the academic-record half of every applicant's aggregate, depressing closing merits the following year.

The NAT Variance

For the NAT stream specifically, the small size of the reserved seat pool causes violent aggregate swings—witnessing a +10% jump one cycle and a -8% drop the next.

The Mental Model

Your stress or anxiety will not alter the numerical outcomes of the cycle. The selection chart functions as a completely deterministic formula once the applicant pool is locked and fixed.

The only productive and logical use of your post-exam waiting period is securing alternative options. Focus your energy on applying to reliable backups and adjusting to university refund timelines, instead of posting redundant "chance me" threads in online discussion forums.

  • If your aggregate is clearly above the safe range, you are probably in a good position.
  • If your aggregate is near the previous closing merit, treat it as entirely uncertain.
  • If your aggregate sits below the buffered calculation, do not wait blindly—execute a backup track immediately.

Quick Summary

FAST does not publish a clean ranked merit list, so most closing merit numbers online are unofficial estimates. Use them carefully, but do not treat them as final truth.

The safest method is to check previous years, focus on your exact campus/program, add a 2 to 3 percent buffer, and keep backup universities ready.
