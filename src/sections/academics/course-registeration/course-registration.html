Course Registration, Add/Drop, Withdrawal & Repeating Courses

Course registration decides what you officially study in a semester. At FAST, this matters because registration, add/drop, withdrawal, repeats, and FYP rules can affect your fee, CGPA, degree plan, and graduation timeline.

Semester Registration

During the Fall and Spring cycles, students must officially enroll in their courses and clear all corresponding tuition balances. The university structure requires students to register for a standard, full-load academic track unless they are constrained by an active academic warning or an approved administrative variance.

1

Clear Arrears: You must fully clear all previous financial dues before the system unlocks your new enrollment window.

2

Load Structuring: Register for the standard normal course load specified by your degree roadmap, unless academic warning limits apply.

3

Freshman Intake: First-semester students are registered directly by the Academic Office to secure their fixed foundation tracks.

4

Continuing Enrollment: All senior cohorts execute their course selections independently through the online portal system.

5

Immediate Settlement: Pay the current semester tuition fees in full immediately after completing your course selections.

Important: Do not delay fee or registration steps casually. Registration problems can affect your semester start and course access.

Adding or Dropping a Course

The add/drop process offers a narrow administrative window at the start of the term for modifying your schedule. It allows students to fix structural roadmap errors, but it operates under strict institutional boundaries:

Two-Week Deadline

All add/drop modifications must be finalized within the first two weeks of the semester.

HOD Authorization

Formally submitting a request requires mandatory HOD approval; it is not an automatic self-service option.

First-Semester Floor

You cannot drop any course during your first semester. The initial curriculum is non-negotiable.

Withdrawing from a Course

Withdrawal is a separate process that happens later in the semester. While dropping a course cleans it from your record early on, a formal withdrawal allows you to step away from a class later in the term without damaging your GPA. It is governed by strict regulations:

  • No First-Semester Exceptions: Course withdrawals are completely disallowed for first-semester students.
  • Guardian Endorsement: Every withdrawal application must be physically countersigned by a parent or guardian.
  • The Terminal Deadline: Applications must be submitted to the HOD before the final day of formal classes.
  • Theory and Lab Coupling: For courses featuring an associated lab component, you must withdraw from both theory and lab sections simultaneously.
  • No Financial Refund: Tuition fees paid for a withdrawn course are completely non-refundable and non-transferable.
Watch out: A withdrawn course may not hurt your GPA like an F, but it can still affect your degree plan, course sequence, and fee.

Repeating a Course: Rules & Transcript Impact

FAST applies a strict replacement policy to manage academic repeats. While it offers a pathway to fix mistakes, it leaves a permanent mark on your student profile:

Mandatory Repeats

You must repeat all failed courses. Students on warning must also repeat passed tracks if their grade falls below the target CGPA.

Grade Replacement

Only the most recent grade counts toward your CGPA calculation—even if your second attempt results in a lower score.

Permanent Notation

An official repeat count notation remains permanently visible on your transcript for every course re-registration.

The Concept Rebuild Strategy: Despite the permanent notation, repeating a foundational gateway course (such as Programming Fundamentals) in the immediate next semester is a proven success pattern. Alumni report that addressing core conceptual gaps early yields a significantly higher final CGPA than coasting through with a borderline, shaky pass.

Final Year Project (FYP) Registration

The Final Year Project represents a major, two-semester milestone. It must be completed sequentially across FYP-I followed by FYP-II, and it cannot be treated as a casual elective:

Student Situation FYP-I Registration Status
All courses passed from semesters 1 to 6 Approved for full enrollment.
Deficiency of exactly one course Allowed, subject to explicit HOD approval.
Deficiency of exactly two courses Allowed, subject to explicit Campus Director approval.
Deficiency of more than two courses Strictly disallowed. Registration is blocked.
Student is under an active Academic Warning Strictly disallowed. Warning statuses block entry to FYP-I.
FYP-II Rule: FYP-II enrollment is only unlocked after you successfully pass and finalize all evaluation criteria for FYP-I.

Common Mistakes Students Make

Most registration problems happen because students delay decisions, misunderstand withdrawal, or repeat courses without understanding the CGPA impact.

  • Thinking add/drop can be done anytime in the semester.
  • Confusing dropping a course with withdrawing from a course.
  • Forgetting that withdrawn course fee is usually not refunded.
  • Repeating a passed course without knowing that the latest grade counts.
  • Ignoring failed foundational courses and then struggling in later semesters.
  • Planning FYP without checking deficiencies and warning status.

Quick Summary

Semester registration requires clearing any past dues and paying your current fees immediately after picking courses. The add/drop window closes after the first two weeks and requires HOD approval, while course withdrawals must be submitted before classes end, require parent signatures, and offer no refunds.

Failed courses must be cleared, but because your latest attempt completely replaces previous grades in your CGPA, repeats require careful planning. Finally, entering FYP-I requires passing your first six semesters, carries a maximum deficiency allowance of two courses with Director approval, and is completely blocked for any student on academic warning.
