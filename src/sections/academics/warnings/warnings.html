Academic Warnings, Probation & When Admission Closes

Academic warning is FAST's way of telling you that your CGPA has fallen below the minimum required level. It is serious, but it is not always the end. The important thing is to understand the warning count, registration limits, and how to recover before the situation becomes dangerous.

The Warning Threshold

A warning is issued at the end of a semester if your CGPA is below the minimum required for your program. This can happen after Fall, Spring, or even Summer.

BS / BBA

Minimum CGPA required is usually 2.00.

MS / MBA

Minimum CGPA required is usually 2.50.

PhD

Minimum CGPA required is usually 3.00.

Simple meaning: If your CGPA falls below your program's minimum graduation requirement, you enter the warning zone.

How Warning Count Works

Warning count is the number that tracks how many times your CGPA stayed below the required minimum. If your CGPA improves back to the minimum level or above, the warning count usually resets to zero.

But if your CGPA remains below the minimum after another semester, your warning count increases. When the warning count reaches 3, admission can be closed.

1

Your CGPA falls below the required minimum, so you receive an academic warning.

2

You need HOD approval to register for the next semester.

3

If your CGPA is still below the minimum after the next semester, warning count increases.

4

If your CGPA improves to the required level, warning count resets to zero.

5

If warning count reaches 3, your admission may be closed.

Danger point: Warning count 3 is the serious stage. Do not wait until then to fix your study plan.

Registration Rules While Under Warning

Students under warning do not register like normal students. Your course registration becomes more controlled because the university wants you to fix weak courses first.

  • You must register for all courses graded F or U.
  • You should register for courses graded D, D+, or C- if they are hurting your CGPA.
  • You should usually register for no more than five courses.
  • If warning count is 2, you usually must not register for any new course.
  • Parents or guardians may be called for a meeting with the HOD.
  • Written HOD approval may be required for add, drop, or withdrawal decisions.
  • Students on scholarships may be required to take full load depending on scholarship rules.
Important: When you are under warning, your priority is not speed. Your priority is recovery.

What Warning Count 2 Means

Warning count 2 is a red-alert stage. At this point, the goal is usually to stop the damage and repair your CGPA. That is why students at this stage may be restricted from taking new courses.

Instead of rushing forward, the better move is to repeat failed or weak courses, reduce overload, and focus on getting your CGPA back above the required minimum.

Do Not Rush

Taking too many courses can make recovery harder.

Fix Weak Courses

Repeating failed or low-grade courses can improve your CGPA.

Get Approval

Course decisions usually need proper HOD approval.

A Bad First Year Is Recoverable

A weak first year feels scary, but it does not automatically destroy your degree. Later semesters carry enough credit hours that a student can still improve the cumulative average with consistent performance.

The key is not just “study harder.” The key is to change the method. If the old method caused the warning, repeating the same method with more stress usually does not fix the problem.

  • Identify which courses damaged your CGPA the most.
  • Repeat foundational courses if the concepts are weak.
  • Reduce overload where possible.
  • Ask seniors for teacher-specific and course-specific advice.
  • Track your CGPA mathematically instead of guessing.
  • Use quizzes and assignments to build marks early.
  • Do not wait for finals to recover the whole semester.
Recovery mindset: A bad semester is a warning signal, not your identity. Fix the system that produced it.

When Admission Is Closed

Admission closure is the stage where your enrollment at the university ends. Academic warning count reaching 3 is one major reason, but it is not the only possible reason.

Reason What It Means
Third academic warning Warning count reaches 3 because CGPA was not restored to the required minimum.
Suspended registration not restored The student fails to fix a suspended registration situation.
Failure to rejoin after freeze The student does not continue after freezing admission for two consecutive semesters.
False documents Admission was obtained using incorrect or fake documents.
Disciplinary action The university closes admission because of a serious disciplinary case.
Student's own request The student requests closure for personal reasons.
Eligibility failure A new admission is closed if awaited result does not meet eligibility criteria.
Most important case: For regular students, the big academic danger is the third warning. Treat warning count 1 seriously so you never reach warning count 3.

How to Avoid Reaching Warning Count 3

Avoiding admission closure requires early action. The worst mistake is to hide from the problem because of fear or embarrassment.

  • Meet your advisor or HOD early instead of waiting until registration week.
  • Calculate how much SGPA you need to bring CGPA back above the minimum.
  • Repeat failed courses as soon as possible.
  • Do not overload yourself with difficult new courses while under warning.
  • Attend classes and labs because F/A can destroy recovery.
  • Focus on high-credit courses because they affect CGPA more.
  • Build a realistic weekly study routine, not a motivational one-day plan.

Quick Summary

Academic warning happens when your CGPA falls below the minimum required level: usually 2.00 for BS/BBA, 2.50 for MS/MBA, and 3.00 for PhD. If your CGPA stays below the minimum, warning count increases.

If warning count reaches 3, admission can be closed. But a bad first year is recoverable if you change your study method, repeat weak courses properly, reduce overload, and bring your CGPA back above the required minimum.
