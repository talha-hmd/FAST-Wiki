Academic Warnings, Probation & When Admission Closes

Academic warning is FAST's way of telling you that your CGPA has fallen below the minimum required level. It is serious, but it is not always the end. The important thing is to understand the warning count, registration limits, and how to recover before the situation becomes dangerous.

The Warning Threshold

An official academic warning is generated automatically at the close of every standard semester term (including the Summer session) if a student's cumulative GPA drops below the absolute minimum floor required for graduation.

BS / BBA

Minimum CGPA required is strictly 2.00.

MS / MBA

Minimum CGPA required is strictly 2.50.

PhD

Minimum CGPA required is strictly 3.00.

Any student carrying an active warning status is blocked from standard self-service registration and requires formal, explicit HOD approval to build their next course sheet. If the CGPA remains below the required floor at the end of the next active semester, the Warning Count increases by one. However, if the student pulls their cumulative metric back up to or above the graduation floor, the Warning Count resets completely to zero.

Registration Rules While Under Warning

Course registration under a warning status follows a tightly managed corrective framework designed to remediate weak grade points immediately rather than advancing blindly:

  • Mandatory F/U Registration: You must prioritize and re-register for all courses where you received an F (Fail) or U (Unsatisfactory) grade.
  • Recommended Grade Remediation: You should actively re-register for prior courses where you earned a D, D+, or C- to swap out low-weight metrics.
  • Strict Credit Caps: Total registration should strictly encompass no more than five courses to prevent academic overload.
  • Scholarship Exceptions: Students holding external or internal merit awards (such as OSP, HEC, or FAST institutional scholarships) are required to register for a complete full load as dictated by their award criteria.
  • Administrative Sign-offs: You must secure a physical, written HOD signature approval for every single add, drop, or course withdrawal attempt.

The Warning Count 2 Red-Alert Stage

Reaching a Warning Count of 2 places you on the precipice of academic dismissal. At this terminal tracking milestone, the university shifts from a policy of guided counseling to absolute operational constraint:

The Warning Count 2 Protocol: Students at this boundary are strictly forbidden from registering for any new courses. The system locks out advanced roadmap progression entirely, forcing the candidate to focus exclusively on repeating past weak or failed modules. Furthermore, parents or guardians are formally called into the university for a mandatory, in-person consultative strategy meeting with the HOD.

The Reality: A Wrecked First Year Is Recoverable

Falling into the warning zone during your first two semesters feels incredibly scary, but it is a historically solvable scenario. Because early semesters represent only a fraction of your total degree path, later credit-hour blocks carry substantial mathematical weight to swing your cumulative average upward.

As former faculty members frequently remind struggling students tracking at a mid-2 baseline after early shocks: "The CGPA at the end of first year can be improved quite easily in the latter years."

In fact, campus records document several elite recovery trajectories—such as candidates starting with a 1.6 first-semester CGPA who rallied to finish with a 3.04 by graduation. The defining differentiator in these turnarounds is never just applying raw, unguided effort within a broken system; it requires completely auditing and restructuring your study methods to match FAST's high-frequency evaluation model.

When Admission Is Closed

Admission closure is the absolute final boundary where a student's enrollment link with FAST is permanently severed. The system enforces zero-tolerance parameters across several academic and administrative thresholds:

Reason for Closure Systemic Rule Check
Third Academic Warning The student's cumulative GPA fails to clear the program floor for three consecutive check periods, triggering automatic dismissal.
Suspension Neglect The student fails to satisfy administrative criteria or clear a suspended registration status within specified timelines.
Consecutive Freeze Overstay The student fails to re-enroll or re-continue classes after freezing their admission for two consecutive semesters.
False Documentation Discovery that an admission was captured using forged, inaccurate, or incomplete credential documentation.
Disciplinary Action A formal recommendation for permanent expulsion or dismissal is issued by the Campus Disciplinary Committee.
Personal Withdrawal Request The student formally files an official personal request to terminate their enrollment due to independent life changes.
Eligibility Filter Failure A provisional fresh admission is automatically terminated if an awaited external prerequisite result falls below baseline entry criteria.

How to Avoid Reaching Warning Count 3

Avoiding admission closure requires early action. The worst mistake is to hide from the problem because of fear or embarrassment.

  • Meet your advisor or HOD early instead of waiting until registration week.
  • Calculate how much SGPA you need to bring CGPA back above the minimum.
  • Repeat failed courses as soon as possible.
  • Do not overload yourself with difficult new courses while under warning.
  • Attend classes and labs because F/A can destroy recovery.
  • Focus on high-credit courses because they affect CGPA more.
  • Build a realistic weekly study routine, not a motivational one-day plan.

Quick Summary

An academic warning triggers immediately if your CGPA drops below core graduation baselines (2.00 for BS/BBA, 2.50 for MS/MBA, and 3.00 for PhD). If your cumulative profile remains below that line across successive semesters, the warning count stacks.

Reaching Warning Count 3 results in automated admission closure. However, early warning shocks can be entirely reversed by executing mandatory grade repetitions, adhering to strict five-course maximum limits, and completely re-engineering your operational study routines to correct early performance gaps cleanly.
